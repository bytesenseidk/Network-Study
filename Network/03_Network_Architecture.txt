Network Architecture
- How networks are structured and how devices are logically connected


==================================================
1. Network Types
==================================================
- Network types describe the scope and scale of a network based on geographic coverage and the devices it connects.
- Different network types are optimized for different distances, performance requirements, and use cases.


LAN (Local Area Network):
- A LAN is a network that connects devices within a limited geographic area such as a home, office, or building.
- LANs typically use Ethernet or WiFi and provide high-speed communication between devices like computers, printers, and servers.
- Most internal corporate networks and home networks are LANs.


WAN (Wide Area Network):
- A WAN connects multiple LANs across large geographic distances, often spanning cities, countries, or continents.
- The Internet is the largest example of a WAN.
- WANs typically rely on infrastructure provided by telecommunications providers and use technologies such as fiber optics, leased lines, and satellite links.


MAN (Metropolitan Area Network):
- A MAN covers a larger area than a LAN but smaller than a WAN, typically spanning a city or metropolitan region.
- MANs are often operated by municipalities, universities, or large service providers to connect multiple local networks within a city.


PAN (Personal Area Network):
- A PAN is a very small network designed to connect devices around a single person.
- Examples include Bluetooth connections between smartphones, wireless headphones, smartwatches, or other personal devices.
- PANs typically operate over very short distances.


==================================================
2. Network Topologies
==================================================
- Network topology describes the physical or logical layout of how devices are connected in a network.
- Different topologies affect reliability, scalability, performance, and fault tolerance.


Star Topology:
- In a star topology, all devices connect to a central device such as a switch or hub.
- This is the most common topology used in modern Ethernet networks.
- If one device fails, it does not affect the rest of the network, but failure of the central device can disrupt the entire network.


Ring Topology:
- In a ring topology, each device connects to two neighboring devices, forming a circular path for data.
- Data travels around the ring until it reaches the destination device.
- Older networking technologies such as Token Ring used this topology.


Bus Topology:
- In a bus topology, all devices share a single communication cable called the backbone.
- Each device listens for traffic on the shared medium.
- Bus networks were common in early Ethernet networks but are rarely used today due to collision and scalability issues.


Mesh Topology:
- In a mesh topology, devices are connected to multiple other devices, creating multiple paths for data to travel.
- This increases redundancy and reliability because if one path fails, traffic can be rerouted through another path.
- Mesh networks are common in backbone networks and wireless mesh systems.


Hybrid Topology:
- A hybrid topology combines multiple network topology types within the same network.
- For example, a network may use star topology within buildings while connecting those networks together using mesh or hierarchical designs.


==================================================
3. Network Segmentation
==================================================
- Network segmentation is the practice of dividing a network into smaller, isolated segments to improve performance, organization, and security.
- Segmentation limits broadcast traffic and helps prevent attackers from easily moving across the entire network.
- Common segmentation techniques include VLANs, subnets, and firewall-controlled zones.
