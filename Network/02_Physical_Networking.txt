Physical Networking
- How data physically travels through cables, wireless signals, and network interfaces


==================================================
1. Transmission Medium
==================================================
- The transmission medium is the physical path that carries data signals between devices.
- It can be a wired medium such as copper or fiber optic cables, or a wireless medium using radio waves.
- The characteristics of the medium affect bandwidth, latency, interference, and maximum transmission distance.


==================================================
2. Copper Cabling
==================================================
- Copper cables transmit data using electrical signals. They are the most common medium in local area networks.
- Examples include twisted-pair Ethernet cables such as Cat5e, Cat6, and Cat7.
- Copper is inexpensive and easy to install but is limited in transmission distance and susceptible to electromagnetic interference (EMI).


==================================================
3. Fiber Optics
==================================================
- Fiber optic cables transmit data as pulses of light through thin strands of glass or plastic.
- They support extremely high bandwidth and very long transmission distances compared to copper cables.
- Fiber is resistant to electromagnetic interference and is widely used in backbone networks, data centers, and Internet infrastructure.


==================================================
4. Wireless Transmission
==================================================
- Wireless networking transmits data using radio waves instead of physical cables.
- Technologies such as WiFi, Bluetooth, and cellular networks rely on antennas to send and receive signals through the air.
- Wireless networks provide mobility but are more susceptible to interference, signal attenuation, and security risks.


==================================================
5. Signal Types
==================================================
- Signals are the physical representation of data as it travels through a medium.
- A signal can represent binary data (1s and 0s) through variations in voltage, light intensity, or radio frequency.
- Signals may be digital or analog depending on how the data is encoded.


==================================================
6. Digital Signals
==================================================
- Digital signals represent data using discrete values, typically binary states (0 and 1).
- In networking, digital signals are used by computers and networking equipment to transmit structured data across networks.
- Digital signals are generally less susceptible to noise and easier to process by modern devices.


==================================================
7. Analog Signals
==================================================
- Analog signals represent data as continuous waves that vary in amplitude, frequency, or phase.
- Traditional telecommunication systems such as early telephone networks used analog signals.
- Modern networks typically convert analog signals into digital form for processing and transmission.


==================================================
8. Modulation
==================================================
- Modulation is the process of encoding digital data onto a carrier signal so it can be transmitted over a medium.
- This involves altering properties of the carrier wave such as amplitude, frequency, or phase.
- Wireless communication, DSL, and cable Internet all rely on modulation techniques to carry data efficiently.


==================================================
9. Network Interface Card (NIC)
==================================================
- A Network Interface Card is the hardware component that allows a device to connect to a network.
- It provides the physical interface between the computer and the transmission medium (Ethernet cable or wireless signal).
- Each NIC has a unique MAC address used for identification at the data link layer.
