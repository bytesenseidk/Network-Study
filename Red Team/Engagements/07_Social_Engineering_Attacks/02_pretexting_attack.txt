Pretexting Attack
- A social engineering technique where an attacker fabricates a believable scenario to manipulate victims into revealing sensitive information or performing actions.

1. Overview
==================================================
Pretexting is a form of social engineering where an attacker creates a fabricated identity or scenario (the pretext) to gain the trust of a victim.

Unlike phishing, which typically relies on mass messages, pretexting is often:

- targeted
- interactive
- conversational

Attackers impersonate trusted individuals or roles such as:

- IT support
- company executives
- HR personnel
- vendors or service providers

The goal is to convince the victim to reveal information or perform an action that benefits the attacker.


==================================================
2. Attack Scenario
==================================================
An attacker researches an organization and identifies potential targets.

The attacker then contacts an employee while pretending to be someone with authority or legitimacy.

Examples include:

- pretending to be IT support requesting a password reset
- posing as HR requesting employee verification
- impersonating a vendor requesting access to systems

Because the story sounds legitimate, the victim may comply with the request.


==================================================

==================================================

OSINT Tools

Used to impersonate trusted phone numbers.

==================================================
5. Step-by-Step Execution
==================================================
Step 1 — Gather Information

The attacker collects information about the target organization.

Examples include:

- employee names
- departments
- company structure
- internal systems


Step 2 — Create the Pretext

The attacker prepares a believable scenario.

Example pretext:

"I’m from the IT department and we’re performing security maintenance."


Step 3 — Contact the Target

The attacker contacts the victim by phone, email, or messaging.


Step 4 — Build Trust

The attacker references internal information to appear legitimate.


Step 5 — Request Information

Examples:

- passwords
- login codes
- internal documents
- system access


Step 6 — Use the Obtained Information

The attacker uses the collected information to gain unauthorized access.


==================================================
6. Example Pretexting Scenario
==================================================
Reconnaissance

Attacker identifies an employee in the finance department.


Pretext Creation

Attacker impersonates an IT support technician.


Initial Contact

The attacker calls the employee claiming there is a security issue.


Information Request

The attacker asks the employee to verify their login credentials.


Access

The attacker uses the stolen credentials to access internal systems.


==================================================
7. Expected Results
==================================================
Successful pretexting attacks allow attackers to:

- obtain sensitive information
- bypass security controls
- gain system access
- assist further social engineering attacks

These attacks are particularly dangerous because they exploit human trust.


==================================================
8. Defensive Considerations
==================================================
Organizations can reduce the risk of pretexting attacks through several measures.

Security Awareness Training

Teach employees how social engineering attacks work.

Verification Procedures

Require identity verification for sensitive requests.

Least Privilege

Limit access to sensitive information.

Incident Reporting

Encourage employees to report suspicious requests.

Communication Policies

Define official procedures for IT or administrative requests.

10. Questions & Answers
==================================================

Question:
What is a pretexting attack?

Answer:
A social engineering attack where an attacker invents a believable scenario to manipulate a victim into revealing information or performing actions.

--------------------------------------------------

Question:
How does pretexting differ from phishing?

Answer:
Pretexting usually involves direct interaction with a victim, while phishing typically uses mass messages.

--------------------------------------------------

Question:
What roles do attackers often impersonate during pretexting attacks?

Answer:
IT staff, company executives, HR personnel, or external vendors.

--------------------------------------------------

Question:
What is the primary weakness exploited by pretexting attacks?

Answer:
Human trust.

--------------------------------------------------

Question:
What is an effective defense against pretexting?

Answer:
Security awareness training combined with strict identity verification procedures.
