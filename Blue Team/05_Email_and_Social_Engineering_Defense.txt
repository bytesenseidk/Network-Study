1. Overview
==================================================
Social engineering attacks target human behavior rather than technical vulnerabilities.

Attackers manipulate users into:

- revealing credentials
- downloading malware
- executing malicious actions
- granting unauthorized access

Email is the most common delivery method for social engineering attacks.

These attacks are dangerous because they bypass many traditional security controls.

Common examples include:

- phishing emails
- business email compromise (BEC)
- malicious attachments
- credential harvesting websites
- USB baiting attacks

Defending against social engineering requires both technical controls and user awareness.


==================================================
2. Common Social Engineering Attacks
==================================================

Phishing

Fraudulent emails designed to steal credentials or deliver malware.


Spear Phishing

Highly targeted phishing attacks against specific individuals.


Business Email Compromise (BEC)

Impersonating executives or vendors to request financial transfers.


Pretexting

Creating a believable scenario to manipulate victims into revealing information.


Malicious Attachments

Emails containing malware disguised as documents.


USB Baiting

Planting malicious USB devices for victims to connect to their computers.


Credential Harvesting

Fake login portals designed to capture usernames and passwords.


==================================================
3. Email Security Controls
==================================================
Organizations deploy several technologies to secure email systems.

Spam Filtering

Automatically detects and blocks suspicious emails.


Email Security Gateways

Scan messages for malicious attachments and links.


URL Rewriting

Inspect links before allowing users to access them.


Attachment Sandboxing

Execute attachments in isolated environments to detect malware.


Domain Authentication

Verify sender authenticity using email security protocols.


==================================================
4. Email Authentication Technologies
==================================================
Modern email systems use several authentication methods to prevent spoofing.

SPF (Sender Policy Framework)

Defines which servers are allowed to send emails for a domain.


DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)

Digitally signs email messages to verify authenticity.


DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication)

Enforces SPF and DKIM policies and reports spoofing attempts.


These technologies help prevent attackers from impersonating trusted domains.


==================================================
5. User Awareness Training
==================================================
Human awareness is one of the most important defenses.

Organizations should train employees to recognize:

- suspicious email senders
- urgent requests for credentials
- unexpected attachments
- links to login pages
- requests for sensitive information

Employees should be encouraged to:

- verify suspicious requests
- report suspicious emails
- avoid opening unknown attachments


==================================================
6. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
==================================================
Multi-factor authentication significantly reduces the risk of credential theft.

Even if attackers obtain user credentials, MFA requires an additional authentication factor.

Examples include:

- mobile authentication apps
- hardware tokens
- biometric authentication

MFA is one of the most effective defenses against phishing attacks.


==================================================
7. Email Monitoring and Detection
==================================================
Security teams monitor email activity for suspicious patterns.

Indicators include:

Large Phishing Campaigns

Many users receiving similar suspicious emails.


Unusual Login Activity

Users logging in from unexpected locations.


Email Forwarding Rules

Attackers creating forwarding rules after account compromise.


Suspicious Attachments

Files containing macros or executable content.


SIEM platforms often monitor email logs and generate alerts.


==================================================
8. Example Detection Scenario
==================================================

Alert Trigger

Email security gateway detects a suspicious login page link.


Log Analysis

Multiple employees received a phishing email.


Investigation

The email domain closely resembles the legitimate company domain.


Response

The phishing domain is blocked and users are warned.


Outcome

Credential theft is prevented.


==================================================
9. Defending Against Malicious USB Attacks
==================================================
Organizations should implement controls to prevent USB-based attacks.

Device Control Policies

Restrict unauthorized USB devices.


Endpoint Security

Detect suspicious USB device behavior.


Disable Auto-Run

Prevent automatic execution of files.


Security Awareness

Train employees not to plug unknown USB devices into computers.


Physical Security

Prevent attackers from placing devices in sensitive locations.


==================================================
10. Incident Response for Phishing
==================================================
When phishing attacks occur, organizations should respond quickly.

Response steps include:

Identify affected users

Reset compromised credentials

Remove malicious emails from inboxes

Block malicious domains

Investigate attacker activity

Notify affected users


Rapid response helps prevent further compromise.
